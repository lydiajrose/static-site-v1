Digital Literacy: A personal history

The Beginning of Digital Literacy

From the age of two I was addicted to video games. It was almost second nature for me to watch as my brother blazed through side scrollers, unlocked every character and stage of his fighter games, and beat every computer player he came across. On the occasions he let me play with him, I was always fumbling around trying to do any basic moves in each new game and on each new console. Though I was never able to beat my brother, I was usually able to competently play each game after a couple tries.

It wasn't really until I was in middle school that I got hands on experience using typical PC software like Word, Microsoft Paint, Powerpoint, etc. and it was the skills that I learned while playing video games with my brother that allowed me to adapt to these new technologies more quickly than I would have otherwise. It might have also helped that I could always ask my electrical-engineer father for help if I had any questions. If anything, that is one of the greatest skills I've learned: how to find people who can help you and how to ask them for help.

I saw this particularly at my internship at Best Buy. You always have to learn new technologies at every job, but it's always difficult when you're trying to document technologies that you have no experience using. Not only do you need to toy the technology itself, you have to ask a lot of questions and do some outside research on your own in order to make the best material you can.

Where Do We Go From Here?

Since middle school, I've become more adept at using the longer lasting technologies that I began to learn at that time. I've also dabbled in programming in going from a Computer Science major to a Technical Writing major and have had the opportunity to take classes and participate in internships that taught me new programs such as Adobe software, WordPress, and GitHub. I am constantly learning new technologies and I doubt that will change any time soon.
